Jewellery · Diamonds & Alternatives
Diamonds &
Alternatives
There are four types of sparkly stones you'll come across at the thrift store that can look like a diamond. Here's how to tell them apart — and what actually matters.
Mined Diamond
Carbon compressed underground over 1–3 billion years. Hardness: 10 Mohs — the hardest natural substance on earth.

Mined diamonds have been linked to serious human rights abuses. The Kimberley Process was created in the early 2000s to certify "conflict-free" stones, but in 2025 it is widely considered a narrow and inadequate measure. Alex Cuadros' The Myth of Conflict-Free Diamonds in the NYT is worth reading.

Mined diamond prices dropped roughly 20% from 2024–2025, largely due to the rise of lab grown diamonds.
Lab Grown Diamond
Chemically, structurally and visually identical to a mined diamond — same carbon crystal, same hardness, same refractive index, same density (3.52 g/cm³). Made in weeks using HPHT or CVD instead of billions of years underground.

The GIA grades both identically. The only way to tell them apart requires specialized spectroscopy equipment. Lab grown diamonds are laser-inscribed "Laboratory-Grown," visible under a loupe.
"Anyone who claims they can visually tell the difference between a mined and lab grown diamond is incorrect — that's marketing, not gemology."
Moissanite
Silicon carbide — a completely different material. Slightly softer at 9.25 Mohs. Has double refraction — look at the back facet edges through a loupe and you'll see them appear doubled. Also tends to throw more rainbow fire. Originally discovered in a meteor crater by Henri Moissan.
Cubic Zirconia
Zirconium dioxide. Softer at 8.5 Mohs, noticeably heavier in the hand, throws excessive orange and blue flash fire in sunlight, and is almost always flawless inside — a real diamond almost always has inclusions. Very common at thrift.

The value of a ring has nothing to do with the value of what it means to the person wearing it. A cubic zirconia can mean everything. A mined diamond can mean nothing.

Gemstones · Turquoise
Turquoise
One of the most commonly faked stones at thrift stores. The real thing is beautiful — and once you know what to look for, the fakes become obvious.
The Real Thing
Turquoise ranges from sky blue to greenish-blue with a matrix — the web-like brown or black lines across the surface. That's the host rock it formed inside. No two pieces will ever have the same pattern.

Stabilized turquoise is real turquoise treated with resin to make it harder. This is how most turquoise jewellery is made today — natural untreated turquoise is soft at 5–6 Mohs and can crack. Still real.

Reconstituted turquoise is turquoise dust mixed with resin and pressed into shape — a step down, but still contains real material.
Howlite — The Common Dupe
Howlite is a white stone with grey veining that, when dyed blue, looks a lot like turquoise at first glance.
"It almost looks like the veins are coloured in with a marker. Once you see it in real life you won't be able to unsee it."
The veining. Howlite has evenly spaced, uniform veins — too tidy to be natural.
The colour. Howlite dye is consistent or unnaturally bright. Real turquoise has variation — lighter in spots, deeper in others.
The vein colour. Howlite veins look painted. Real turquoise matrix is brown or black host rock.

Gemstones · Amber
Amber
Fossilized tree resin millions of years old — and one of the easier gems to test right there in the store.
The Real Thing
Real amber feels warm to the touch — you won't get the cold sensation you'd feel from glass or plastic. It's also very lightweight for its size.

When amber contains a preserved insect, part of an insect, or plant material, that's called an inclusion. Amber preserves ancient life perfectly and can be over 100 million years old.
"Real amber feels warm. Glass and plastic feel cold. That's your first clue before you even look closely."
The Salt Water Test
Mix about 2 tablespoons of salt into a cup of water. Real amber floats. Fake plastic amber sinks. Simple and satisfying.
Fakes to Know
Most common fake is plastic. Copal is younger tree resin that hasn't fully fossilized — looks almost identical but gets sticky with a drop of acetone. Real amber won't react.
Inclusion red flag
If you see "amber" with a perfectly preserved insect that looks a little too perfect and too centred — be suspicious. Real inclusions look naturally positioned, not staged.

Gemstones · Jade
Jade
One of the most culturally significant and widely imitated stones in the world. Two completely different minerals are both called jade.
Jadeite vs Nephrite
Jadeite (6.5–7 Mohs) is rarer and more valuable — imperial jadeite, a vivid translucent emerald green, is one of the most expensive gemstones on earth. Nephrite (6–6.5 Mohs) is more common with a softer or more muted green. Both feel cool and heavy in the hand, slow to warm up. Both are extremely tough — a steel knife should not be able to scratch either.
"Jade is distinctive not only because of its origins but because of the way in which it is both a manifestation and reflection of Chinese culture." — Gu Fang
The Chime Test
Tap two pieces of suspected jade together. Real jade produces a clear chime-like ringing sound. Plastic or glass produces a dull clunk.
Dyed stones. Serpentine, aventurine, glass — dyed green and sold as jade. Colour concentrated in cracks is a giveaway.
Plastic. Warms up quickly. Real jade stays cool for a long time.
The scratch test. Try a hidden spot with a steel knife. Real jade won't scratch.

Gemstones · Pearls
Pearls
Made by mollusks when an irritant gets trapped inside and they coat it with layers of nacre. Kinda gross. Genuinely beautiful.
Natural vs Cultured
A natural pearl forms when an irritant enters on its own. A cultured pearl is created when a human places the irritant there intentionally. Both are completely real pearls — cultured does not mean fake.
The Tooth Test
Gently rub the pearl against the biting edge of your front teeth.

Real pearl: feels slightly gritty or sandy — thousands of layers of nacre.
Fake pearl: completely smooth — glass or plastic beads coated with pearlescent paint.
"If you don't want to recreate eating a candy necklace in front of the jewellery counter, check the drill holes instead."
The Drill Hole Test
Real pearls have clean drill holes — you may see layers of nacre inside. Fake pearls often show chipped or flaking coating around the hole where paint peels away from the bead underneath.

Cameras · Section 1
SLR vs
Rangefinder
These are the two camera types you'll find most at thrift. Knowing the difference takes ten seconds and can mean the difference between a $15 shelf piece and a $200 resell.
"The hump on top is the tell. SLRs have it. Rangefinders don't."
How to tell them apart
The prism hump. That raised pentaprism on top of an SLR is the fastest visual tell. Rangefinders are flat-topped.
The viewfinder. On a rangefinder, the viewfinder window sits to the side. On an SLR, you look through the lens.
Lens size. SLR lenses are generally larger. Rangefinder lenses are more compact.

Accessories · Brooches
Brooches
Brooches are coming back in style and the thrift store is full of hidden gems. Vintage brooches can contain real diamonds and precious stones — and rare pieces like the art deco "Rabbit MD" by Raymond Yard have sold for over $44,000 at auction.
Dating by Pin Type
The clasp on the back of a brooch is one of the fastest ways to date your find. Flip it over before anything else.
Clasp typeEra
C-clasp
Simple C-shaped wire, no lock — pre-1940s
Trombone clasp
Long tube mechanism — 1890s to 1940s
Rollover safety
Modern locking pin — post-1940s
Gems & Rhinestones
It's not just what the stones are made of — it's how they're attached. Prong-set rhinestones (held in metal prongs like a real gem) indicate a higher quality piece than stones that are simply glued on. Run your fingernail lightly over the surface and you'll feel the prongs.
Enamel Work
Enamel on a brooch means coloured glass has been fused onto metal at extremely high temperatures — powdered glass melted into place. The result is smooth, vivid colours that never fade or wear off like paint. When you see bright jewel-like blocks of colour separated by thin metal lines, you're likely looking at enamel.

There are two main techniques:
"Think of cloisonné like a tiny stained glass window laid flat — the metal wires are the leading, and the colour fills the spaces between."
Cloisonné. Thin metal wires (cloisons) are soldered onto the surface to create cells, then each cell is filled with coloured glass paste and fired in a kiln.
Champlevé. Works in reverse — the metalworker carves troughs directly into the metal surface, fills them with glass paste and fires it. The raised metal acts as the border between colours.
How to Spot Enamel
Hold it to the light. Real enamel has a glassy depth and slight translucency — it catches light almost like a gemstone.
Run your fingernail across it. Enamel feels smooth and glass-like, sitting level with or just slightly below the metal borders. Cool to the touch.
Check the edges. On cloisonné you'll see fine wire partitions sitting on the surface. On champlevé the colour is flush inside the metal itself.
Feel the weight. Enamel brooches feel heavier than expected — metal plus actual glass. Painted brooches feel noticeably lighter.
Look at the damage. Enamel chips with a sharp glassy fracture, revealing bare metal underneath. Paint or resin just scratches or flakes off.
Check the back. Real enamel pieces often show fire marks from the kiln process on the reverse.
A note from Alex
A great brooch that you love is a great brooch whether it's enamel or not — but sometimes it's fun to inspect just to learn more about what you're holding!

Accessories · Hosiery
Hosiery
One of my absolute favourite things to thrift. Vintage designer tights range from $0.50 to $4 and are one of the most underrated styling tools in the thrift store — patterns, colours, and textures that can completely transform clothes you already own.
Why They're Worth Hunting
During the 80s, 90s and into the 2000s, tights were much more mainstream — especially in formal settings. Chanel, Gucci, Dior and Versace all produced branded hosiery lines for department stores, and these regularly show up unopened and unused at the thrift store. Post-2010 bare legs became more fashionable and many of those designer lines disappeared along with the demand — which means more for us.
The Most Important Sizing Rule
Unlike pants, tight sizing is based primarily on height rather than circumference. This is something a lot of people don't know and it makes a big difference.
"Size A tights have a better chance of fitting my cat Bea's legs than mine. I always size up."
If you have long legs, size up — always. A size too small means the crotch ends up at your kneecaps. A size too large is much more forgiving.

How to Shop Them at Thrift
Hosiery is often bundled into plastic bags with multiple pairs mixed together, which makes it hard to see what's inside. I think of this as a feature — higher chance of a fun find. Some stores have open baskets you can dig through instead.

I always spend time carefully looking through the baggies or digging through the baskets because I regularly find designer tights for less than what a basic pair at the drugstore costs. If you don't wear skirts or dresses, consider knee highs — same great finds, different format.
Pro tip
Check the packaging! Unopened tights in original packaging are common at thrift stores and still in perfect condition. Designer names are printed right on the package.

Art & Prints
Art & Prints · ID Tests
How to ID
Art at Thrift
Three tests you can do in the store with nothing but your phone, your fingernail, and ideally a loupe. Together they'll tell you a lot about what you're holding.
The Raking Light Test
Tilt the piece back and hold your phone flashlight at a very low angle — nearly parallel to the surface. On an original painting, the light will create shadows from raised brushwork. On a print, the surface will reflect light uniformly with no shadow variation at all.
"Hold your phone torch nearly parallel to the surface. Brushwork casts shadows. Prints don't."
The Fingernail Test
I personally don't love this test because it can damage artwork — but it's commonly used. Gently run your fingertip across an unglazed surface. On an original painting, you can feel the ridges of dried paint. A print will feel uniformly smooth. On a screenprint you may feel slightly raised texture from layered ink, but it's subtle.
The 10x Loupe Test
This is where a loupe earns its place. Reproductions printed commercially show a regular grid of tiny CMYK dots forming rosette patterns — flower-like clusters where overlapping dot screens create circular arrangements. Standard commercial printing uses 150–175 lines per inch.

Practice at home: put your loupe on a cereal box and you'll immediately see the dot grid. Then you'll know exactly what to look for at the store.
Modern giclée prints
High-quality digital prints (giclée) use stochastic screening — randomly dispersed dots at 1,800+ DPI. Individual dots may be invisible under 10x. Look for extremely uniform, smooth appearance with no brush texture. You may need 20x–30x magnification to spot the faint speckled pattern.
Plate Marks (Etchings)
Intaglio prints — etchings and engravings — should have a plate mark: an indented rectangular ridge in the paper around the image, caused by the metal plate pressing into the paper during printing. You can feel the edges with your fingertip. If a piece looks like an etching but has no plate mark, it's most likely a reproduction.

Art & Prints
Art & Prints · Print Types
Types of Prints
Knowing how a print was made tells you everything about its value, rarity, and age. Here's a field guide to the types you'll encounter.
Offset Lithograph Most Common
Commercial printing. Under magnification you'll see a uniform, regular grid of CMYK halftone dot patterns forming rosettes — perfectly aligned with mechanical precision. The surface is completely flat. This is what most posters, reproductions, and mass-market prints are. Learning to recognise this dot pattern is the single most useful print ID skill you can develop.
Original Lithograph
The artist draws directly on limestone with a greasy crayon (technique invented 1796). Under magnification the result is irregular stippling mimicking limestone grain — NOT a regular grid. Has a slightly waxy quality with no halftone dots. Genuinely rare and historically significant.
"Practice your loupe on a cereal box at home. Once you know the CMYK dot grid by sight, you'll spot it instantly at the store."
Screenprint / Serigraph
Ink pushed through a mesh screen with a squeegee, one colour at a time. The ink is thick, opaque, and visibly layered — you can feel the layered thickness. Under magnification: fine crosshatch mesh pattern in solid colour areas. No halftone dots. The quintessential Pop Art medium — Warhol and Lichtenstein both worked in screenprint.
Giclée
Term coined 1991 for high-resolution digital inkjet at 1,800+ DPI on archival paper or canvas. Uses stochastic random dots rather than a halftone grid, often on heavy cotton rag paper. The hardest reproduction to identify with a 10x loupe — you may need 20x–30x magnification.
Etching
Acid-bitten metal plate process dating from around 1500. Look for fluid, spontaneous lines with slight ink variation. Must have a plate mark — an indented ridge in the paper around the image. Rembrandt elevated etching to a high art form.
Woodcut
Relief printing from a carved wood block, dating from around 1400. Has a bold, graphic quality. May show wood grain texture in large areas. Japanese ukiyo-e prints are woodcuts.

Art & Prints · Signatures & Editions
Signatures &
Edition Numbers
A real pencil signature can make a significant difference to the value of a print. Here's how to tell if it's genuine — and how to decode edition markings.
Real vs Printed Signature
Think of a pencil signature as sitting on top of the paper surface. When you run your fingernail very gently over it, you can feel its texture and presence. And remember the sheen that pencils leave on paper from school? Under a light source, you can see that same graphite shimmer on a real signature.

A printed signature will show the same dot pattern as the rest of the image. Put your loupe on it — if you see halftone dots running through the signature, it was printed not signed.
"Real pencil signatures have graphite sheen under light, and you can feel them slightly raised under your fingernail. Printed signatures have CMYK dots just like everything else."
Edition Numbers Explained
What does "12/50" written in the corner mean? It means this is print #12 from a total edition of 50. Smaller editions (under 100) are generally rarer and more valuable.

Art & Prints · Check the Back
Always Check
the Back
The back of a piece of art can tell you as much as the front — sometimes more. Here's what to look for.
Gallery Labels & Stickers
Some thrifters peel back the dust covers on the back of framed art to see what lies behind it. I'm honestly undecided on whether this is okay to do — but I want to give you all the information so you can decide for yourself.

People are looking for gallery stickers, auction lot numbers, inventory labels, and exhibition labels. A label from a notable gallery can be a great starting point for researching your find. Keep in mind though: stickers can be faked and anyone can put one on.
The Frame
Frames can actually be more valuable than the art inside them. And removing the original frame for a new one can devalue a piece significantly. The point of thrifting is finding things you enjoy — but it's useful to know before you decide. Well-known framer labels help date and locate a piece.

Art & Prints · Paint Types
Paint Types
Identifying the type of paint used tells you a lot about when a piece was made and how to assess its age. Here's a guide to the main types you'll encounter.
Oil Paint
Used from the 1400s to now. Slow-drying, rich in colour. Look for thick, three-dimensional brushwork called impasto visible when you tilt the canvas under light. Oil paintings often develop craquelure — fine crack networks in the paint — which, counterintuitively, can actually increase a painting's value. White areas may yellow slightly from the oil ageing.
Acrylic Paint
Invented in the 1940s, widely used from the 1960s onward. Dries faster than oil and does not develop the same craquelure. Colours tend to stay more true to themselves over time. Watch out: newer acrylic paintings are sometimes made to look old, but the giveaways are lack of crackling and bright, unfaded colours.
"Bright colours and no craquelure on a supposedly old painting is a big red flag. Acrylic can fake age — but it can't fake cracks."
Watercolour
Applied on absorbent cotton paper rather than canvas. You can often see the paper through the paint, and there is no impasto — it's completely flat. Watercolours are extremely fragile and can fade easily or develop foxing — brown spots from age and moisture.
Gouache
Essentially opaque watercolour with a matte, chalky, velvety surface. Unlike watercolour, you cannot see through the paint to the paper beneath it. Commonly used in illustration and design. When it cracks, it does so in a flat grid-like pattern — quite different from the flowing craquelure of oil.
Tempera
One of the oldest paint types — pigment mixed with egg yolk, predating oil paint entirely. Most common in medieval and early Renaissance art before 1500. Has a smooth, matte, slightly waxy appearance. If you find a painting on wood with these characteristics, pay very close attention — it would be an exciting find.
Encaustic
Pigment mixed into melted beeswax — an ancient technique with a modern revival. Identifiable by its distinctive waxy sheen. The surface can actually be buffed. Rich, textured, layered appearance, primarily used on wood.

Art & Prints · Paintings
Paintings You
May Find
Not every oil painting you find at thrift is what it first appears to be. Here's how to tell mass-produced work, reproductions, and forgeries apart.
Mass-Produced Oil Paintings
These are made on commercial assembly lines where each worker specialises in painting a single element — skies, trees, water — repeatedly all day. They can look completely beautiful and work great in your space! Signs of a mass-produced oil painting include canvas stapled to unfinished stretcher bars and ultra-lightweight frames with no maker's labels. They're not rare, but if you love it, get it.
Canvas Texture Reproductions
These are prints that have a fake brushstroke texture physically embossed onto the surface to mimic an oil painting. The easy test: hold it under raking light and check if the brushstrokes match the image. On a real painting they will. On a reproduction, the texture is a uniform stamped pattern applied over everything regardless of what's underneath — clouds, shadows, faces — all with the same repetitive ridges.
"On a real painting the brushstrokes follow the image. On a canvas-texture reproduction, the raised pattern is stamped everywhere uniformly regardless of what's depicted."
Commonly Forged Artists
Dalí is considered the single most forged artist on the market. He reportedly signed thousands of blank sheets during his lifetime, so a signed Dalí print requires serious research before any claim of value.

Thomas Kinkade work was produced in enormous mass-market editions and is very common at thrift stores. Beautiful if you love it, but not rare.

Frederic Remington bronzes are widely reproduced with the intention of looking authentic. Exercise caution with any unsigned or unlabeled bronzes in his style.
Good to know
It is not impossible to find real work by these artists at the thrift store — it has genuinely happened. These artists are just common enough to warrant extra research before assuming authenticity.

Art & Prints · Signs
Vintage Signs &
Commercial Art
Pre-1960s commercial signage is increasingly collectible and genuinely exciting to find. Here's how to identify and authenticate what you're looking at.
Hand-Painted Signs
Commercial signs made before the 1960s were often painted by skilled sign painters — a serious craft. Signs to look for have slightly uneven lettering with real character to it, visible brushstrokes in the paint, and aged wood or metal as the base material.
Reverse-Painted Glass
A uniquely beautiful technique where paint is applied to the back of glass and viewed through it from the front. Commonly used for signs, pub mirrors, and decorative pieces from the 1800s through the mid-1900s. If you see gold leaf lettering on glass, it may be reverse-painted. The effect has a depth and glow that's impossible to fake with front-painted work.
"Gold leaf lettering on glass with depth you can almost see into — that's reverse-painted glass, and it's worth stopping for."
Enamel & Porcelain Signs
Metal signs with a baked-on enamel coating — extremely durable with a glass-like surface feel. Think vintage Coca-Cola advertising or old gas station and automotive signage. Check for a hefty weight and glassy feel. On authentic enamel signs, chips should reveal bare metal underneath. Reproductions often have very bright colours with no signs of patina — that natural thinprotective layer that forms on metal through oxidation and age.
Tin & Lithographed Metal Signs
Printed using lithography, common from the late 1800s through the 1960s. Key fact: embossed lettering indicates older and generally more valuable. Flat printed lettering indicates more modern. Many reproductions exist and are artificially aged with fake rust and fading. To spot a genuinely vintage piece, look for uneven patina — natural oxidation is much harder to fake convincingly than manufactured aging.

Cameras · Inspection Checklist
Step-by-Step
In-Store Camera Inspection
Check these six things before any camera goes in your cart. Order matters — start with the things that disqualify a camera immediately.
Battery Compartment
Open and look for white, blue or green crusty residue — that's battery corrosion. Deep corrosion that has eaten into the metal contacts likely means the camera no longer works. Do not touch the corrosion; it can cause serious chemical burns. If in doubt, walk away.
Lens Condition
Hold by the sides only — never touch front or rear glass. Hold up to light and look for: fungus (spiderweb-like threads), haze (milky fog), deep scratches, and separation (coating peeling in rings). A little dust is fine. Fungus and heavy haze are reasons to leave it.
Shutter
Open the back and set to B (bulb) mode. Click the shutter while looking through — blades should open and close cleanly. If they're sticky, slow, or uneven, the camera needs a CLA (clean, lubricate, adjust) which costs $75–$200+.
Light Seals
Check the foam strips lining the film door. Gooey black residue or crumbling foam means the seals need replacing — but this is an easy, inexpensive fix: $5–$15 DIY kit or $30–$50 at a shop. Almost every camera from the 1970s–1990s needs new seals eventually.
Meter & Electronics
Borrow a battery from a remote control or radio in the store if it's the same size. Check if the meter needle moves when pointing at bright vs dark. No movement means a dead meter — but in 2026 a free light meter app on your phone works as a substitute!
Film Advance
The lever should move smoothly and cock the shutter with a satisfying click. If it's jammed or sticky, do not force it — internal damage risk. Walk away unless the price is very low.
Brassing (brass showing through worn finish) is purely cosmetic and actually increases value on Leica and Nikon cameras.
Before buying a film camera, confirm the film format is still available. APS film is discontinued. Disc film is dead. 35mm and 120 are your safe bets.

Cameras · Vintage Lenses & Old Glass
Why Old Glass?
Vintage Lenses Are an Incredible Find
A vintage lens for a few dollars could be worth hundreds — and more importantly, could produce images that modern glass literally cannot. Things like swirly bokeh, soft dreamy rendering, gentle vignetting, and unique flare patterns. A $5 lens + a $20 adaptor can be a stunning creative tool.
Do Adaptors Exist?
Almost every vintage mount adapts to modern mirrorless cameras. Canon EF, Sony E, Fuji X and Micro Four Thirds can all accept vintage glass. The adaptor typically costs $15–$60.
MountWhat to know
M42 Screw
Most common vintage mount. Pentax, Zenit, Praktica. Adapts to virtually everything. Home of the famous Helios 44-2.
Pentax K
Used by Pentax from 1975 onward. Widely adaptable, great find.
Canon FD
1971–1987. Cannot adapt to Canon EF/RF without image degradation — but works beautifully on Sony E and Micro 4/3.
Nikon F
1959 onward, one of the longest-running mounts in history. Easily adaptable, older Nikkor glass highly regarded.
Minolta SR/MC/MD
Adapts beautifully to Sony E. Genuinely underrated and often very cheap at the thrift store.
How to Inspect a Vintage Lens
Glass: Hold to light. Look for fungus (spiderweb threads), haze (milky fog), or deep scratches. Dust is normal and fine.
Aperture blades: Look inside the aperture — blades should be dry. Oily shiny blades will cause the aperture to stick.
Focus ring: Turn from closest distance to infinity. Should move smoothly with satisfying resistance.
Aperture ring: Click through stops — each one should click firmly into place.
Filter threads: Check the front for damage. Bent threads can mean the lens was dropped.
What Makes a Lens Valuable
Fast lenses (f/1.4 or f/1.8) are more valuable than slow ones. Leica, Zeiss, and Voigtländer glass can be worth serious money even in average condition. Macro and tilt-shift lenses are worth a second look. Search the lens name on eBay sold listings to see what people are actually paying.
"Old glass is more usable today than it has ever been."

Cameras · Vintage Photography Formats
What You Might Find
Historical Photo Formats at the Thrift Store
Photography is one of the newer mediums in human history — which makes every format a piece of it. Here's how to identify what you're looking at.
Daguerreotype
1840s–1860s
One-of-a-kind images on silver-coated copper plates in small hinged cases with velvet lining. Distinctive mirror-like quality — the image shifts between positive and negative depending on viewing angle.
★ If you find one of these, it is a genuine historical artifact. Extremely rare and valuable.
Ambrotype
1850s–1860s
Similar to daguerreotypes in hinged cases, but made on glass with a dark backing rather than metal. Look at the edge of the image — you can sometimes see where the backing has separated from the glass.
Tintype
1850s–early 1900s
Images on thin iron sheets. Test with a small magnet — tintypes are magnetic, unlike other photo formats. Super durable and still turn up regularly.
Albumen Print
1850s–1900s
First widely used paper photo prints. Distinctive warm sepia or yellowish tone, made using egg whites which gives them a slight sheen. Cabinet cards and cartes de visite (small Victorian portrait cards) are almost always albumen prints.
Cyanotype
1840s–present
Recognizable by their beautiful Prussian blue colour. Used scientifically and artistically. Having a massive modern revival right now.
Silver Gelatin Print
1880s–late 20th c.
What most people picture when they think black and white photography. Vintage examples often have warmth or sepia tone and heavier paper texture than modern prints. Photographer signatures, studio stamps, or exhibition labels can make these genuinely valuable.
Also Worth Knowing
Vernacular photography — shoeboxes of old snapshots, photo booth strips, press photographs — shows up regularly. Press photos sometimes have agency stamps and captions on the back that make them real pieces of photographic history. Lomography prints (distinctive vignetting, light leaks, saturated colors) are collected for both the cameras and the images themselves.
"Historically, photography is a fairly new medium — which makes every format exciting."

How to Use
The Field Guide is a reference tool for thrifters. Each section covers a category of item you might encounter — how to identify it, authenticate it, and know what you're looking at.
Navigating the guide
Use the banners on the home screen to jump to any category. Each category opens into a chapter page with topic cards. Tap a card to read the reference content for that topic.
Content types
Reference articles cover identification, authentication, and what to look for. Tables give you quick lookup data — gemstone tests, size conversions, brand hierarchies. Pull quotes at the bottom of each article are the key thrift tip for that topic.
Brand ID
The Brand ID section covers 39 designers. Each brand page explains the mainline, any diffusion lines, licensed products, and what to look for at the thrift store. Use the search bar at the top to filter by name.
Thrift Tips
Cleaning & Care covers washing protocols for common thrift materials. Leather covers authentication and care. Size Guides give you international size conversions so you can read any label regardless of country of origin.
Bookmark pages
Tap the bookmark icon on any page to save it. Saved pages appear in your Vision Board for quick reference next time you're at the store.
